# They Requested It. I Built It. Nobody Ever Used It.
# 他们提出了需求。我完成了开发。却无人问津。

**Data Science | Hayden Kastens | May 27, 2026**
**数据科学 | Hayden Kastens | 2026年5月27日**

Stakeholders came to us asking for a model. We built a proof of concept. Got the green light. Delivered the model. Weeks of work…all to hear nothing. It’s a tale as old as time, and one that plagues data professionals everywhere, from analysts to ML engineers. So, what happened?
利益相关者向我们提出模型需求。我们完成了概念验证（POC），获得了批准，并交付了模型。数周的心血……最终却石沉大海。这是一个老生常谈的故事，也是困扰着从分析师到机器学习工程师等所有数据从业者的难题。那么，究竟发生了什么？

### Your Model is a Mystery
### 你的模型是个“谜”

Our profession is one rooted in modern computer science and technological advancements. Many of the most powerful solutions at our fingertips are ones that would have been too computationally expensive decades ago. With the reliance on the newest, most capable technical breakthroughs, comes skepticism.
我们的职业根植于现代计算机科学和技术进步。我们手头许多强大的解决方案，在几十年前因计算成本过高而无法实现。然而，对最新、最强技术突破的依赖，也伴随着怀疑。

In data science, we have the ability to create incredibly complex models. My team alone has hundreds of standard features in our feature library that we provide to each new model build. We tune dozens of hyperparameters and use powerful algorithms that iterate over hundreds of runs to maximize predictive performance. This process can create models with incredible accuracy, but it comes at a cost: explainability.
在数据科学领域，我们有能力构建极其复杂的模型。仅我的团队就在特征库中拥有数百个标准特征，供每个新模型构建使用。我们调整数十个超参数，并利用强大的算法进行数百次迭代运行，以最大化预测性能。这一过程能创造出精度惊人的模型，但代价是：可解释性。

There is a fine line between a strong model, and a black box that can’t even be explained by the ones who built it. The explainability-accuracy tradeoff is a big factor in my industry, healthcare, in particular. Customers and stakeholders are often doctors and clinicians. These doctors are used to making clinical judgements using their years of expertise and in-depth knowledge of medicine. While a predictive model may be good at predicting a given outcome, if it cannot be explained well, clinicians will question its trustworthiness.
强大的模型与连开发者都无法解释的“黑箱”之间，只有一线之隔。在我的行业——医疗保健领域，可解释性与准确性之间的权衡尤为重要。客户和利益相关者通常是医生和临床医师。这些医生习惯于利用多年的专业经验和深厚的医学知识做出临床判断。虽然预测模型可能擅长预测特定结果，但如果无法给出合理的解释，临床医生就会质疑其可信度。

If doctors have to choose between a trusted and proven clinical process, or a black box model with cryptic features and inexplainable algorithms, they will likely choose the clinical process every time. So, what can you do to avoid this?
如果医生必须在受信任且经过验证的临床流程，与一个包含晦涩特征和不可解释算法的“黑箱”模型之间做出选择，他们几乎每次都会选择前者。那么，该如何避免这种情况呢？

I find the most success by providing customers with an easily digestible model brief. This is a set of slides that walks the customers through the model. It starts by defining the population of interest, the target, the features, and then ends with proof of concept performance and validation. Along the way, I am sure to define metrics in terms of the business question, putting myself in the customer’s shoes. I avoid pure stat-talk and keep definitions grounded in the customer’s goals.
我发现最有效的方法是为客户提供一份易于消化的“模型简报”。这是一套引导客户了解模型的幻灯片。它从定义目标群体、目标变量和特征开始，最后展示概念验证的性能和验证结果。在此过程中，我确保以业务问题的视角来定义指标，设身处地为客户着想。我避免使用纯粹的统计学术语，并将定义建立在客户的目标之上。

If the model is complex, I stick to high-level explanations of the algorithm and be sure to communicate why I chose such an extensive feature set (or such a simple one). Developing a comprehensive model brief is a crucial step in pulling back the curtain and allowing the customers to understand the model using terms they are familiar with.
如果模型很复杂，我会坚持对算法进行高层级的解释，并确保沟通我选择如此庞大（或如此简单）特征集的原因。开发一份全面的模型简报是揭开神秘面纱的关键一步，让客户能够用他们熟悉的术语理解模型。

### Your Solution Took Too Long
### 你的解决方案耗时太长

Building working models takes time. From the back and forth correspondence with customers, to unexpected twists you didn’t see coming, designing an effective, useful model is not a quick task. And then there’s deployment. That’s a whole process in and of itself. What doesn’t wait patiently is the real world.
构建可用的模型需要时间。从与客户的反复沟通，到意想不到的突发状况，设计一个有效且有用的模型绝非易事。更不用说部署了，那本身就是一个完整的流程。而现实世界是不会耐心等待的。

Customers are living their day-to-day with the tools they already have at their disposal. The tools that existed before they came to you for help. If the model build takes too long, they could abandon the idea altogether, or find creative solutions that don’t involve predictive models. We see this all the time in healthcare.
客户每天都在使用他们现有的工具生活和工作，这些工具在他们向你寻求帮助之前就已经存在了。如果模型构建耗时太长，他们可能会彻底放弃这个想法，或者找到不需要预测模型的创造性解决方案。我们在医疗行业经常看到这种情况。

Stakeholders will request a model. After a few roadblocks (stalled communication from requestors, data access issues, deployment bugs, etc.), weeks of development stretches into months. Finally, you are ready to present findings after everything is validated and working as expected. You attempt to set the meeting and get heartbreak: “We no longer need the model, we figured it out ourselves.”
利益相关者提出模型需求。在经历了一些障碍（需求方沟通停滞、数据访问问题、部署漏洞等）后，数周的开发变成了数月。最终，当你验证了一切并准备好展示成果时，你试图安排会议，却得到了令人心碎的回复：“我们不再需要这个模型了，我们已经自己解决了。”

The hospital setting is a fast-paced environment. Staff doesn’t have time to sit around waiting for months on end. They can and will come up with creative solutions to improve care for their patients, even if that means sacrificing the use of a shiny predictive model.
医院是一个快节奏的环境。员工没有时间坐等数月。他们能够并且确实会想出创造性的解决方案来改善患者护理，即使这意味着放弃使用一个华丽的预测模型。

There is a saying I live by at work: “Don’t let the perfect get in the way of the good”. Build fast. Ideate, refine, review…but always be moving forward. Perfection can prevent you from providing valuable insights. The world moves quickly, and if you get stuck in the build phase for too long, the world will move on without you.
我在工作中信奉一句话：“不要让完美成为优秀的绊脚石”。快速构建。构思、优化、审查……但始终要保持前进。追求完美可能会阻碍你提供有价值的见解。世界变化很快，如果你在构建阶段停留太久，世界就会把你抛在身后。

So, push that v1. If you discover a better way of doing things later on, it can be first on your list of improvements for v2. Some solution is almost always better than no solution. If things are moving slower than planned, then you need to communicate with customers early and often. Keep them posted on your progress, and provide them with a sneak peak to keep them engaged and excited for the final product. Bide your time while you grind to get v1 working and into their hands.
所以，发布 v1 版本吧。如果你后来发现了更好的方法，可以将其列为 v2 改进清单的首位。有解决方案几乎总是好过没有。如果进度比计划慢，你需要尽早且频繁地与客户沟通。让他们随时了解你的进展，并提供预览，以保持他们的参与感和对最终产品的期待。在努力让 v1 版本上线并交付到他们手中时，请耐心等待。

### Your Model Isn’t Easy to Consume
### 你的模型难以使用

Building a good predictive model is only half the battle. In most industries, the stakeholders are busy. In healthcare, the doctors and nurses are absolutely swamped caring for patients. If the data science team comes to an on-floor care team to pitch their newest, most accurate model, but accessing the predictions adds complexity to their workflow and slows them down in the process, the model will never be used.
构建一个好的预测模型只是成功了一半。在大多数行业中，利益相关者都很忙。在医疗领域，医生和护士忙于照顾病人，简直分身乏术。如果数据科学团队向一线护理团队推销他们最新、最精确的模型，但获取预测结果增加了工作流程的复杂性并拖慢了他们的进度，那么这个模型永远不会被使用。

The same can be seen in most industries. Stakeholders want solutions that can increase efficiency, performance, and productivity, not ones that only add complexity to their already busy days. If predictions introduce friction, you are forging a path towards abandonment, not adoption. Serving predictions that are easy to consume can be one of the biggest challenges for data scientists. We may be skilled at creating precise and accurate models, but integrating the model into customers’ daily lives...
这种情况在大多数行业中都很常见。利益相关者需要的是能提高效率、性能和生产力的解决方案，而不是只会给他们本已繁忙的工作增加复杂性的东西。如果预测结果带来了阻力，你就是在走向被抛弃，而不是被采纳。提供易于使用的预测结果，可能是数据科学家面临的最大挑战之一。我们或许擅长创建精确的模型，但如何将模型融入客户的日常生活……
