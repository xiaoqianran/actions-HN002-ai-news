# LEDs’ potential to save our night skies  
# LED灯拯救夜空的潜力  

In the chill of a London spring night, under overcast skies, iconic Trafalgar Square opens around me. Admiral Nelson rises on his pedestal, the National Gallery rests behind, the church of St Martin-in-the-Fields sits nearby. From the 13th century, the site served as the Royal Mews for hawks and then horses. By 1844, it was a public space at the heart of one of the biggest cities in the world.  
在一个伦敦春夜的寒意中，天空阴云密布，标志性的特拉法加广场在我周围展开。纳尔逊海军上将矗立在基座上，国家美术馆静立其后，圣马丁-in-the-Fields教堂 nearby。自13世纪起，这里曾是皇家养鹰场，后来改为马厩。到1844年，它已成为世界最大城市之一的公共空间。  

Despite the square’s presence through that grand sweep of history, it’s not why I’m here. My interest is far more specific: I want to find out what happens to spaces like this when artificial light, specifically from light-emitting diodes (LEDs), intrudes.  
尽管广场承载着如此宏大的历史，但这并非我此行的目的。我的兴趣更为具体：我想了解当人工光，特别是发光二极管（LED）入侵时，像这样的空间会发生什么。  

My companion tonight is Simon Thorp, a local lighting designer, who crouches in the shadows near Nelson’s spire, light meter in hand. “Two lux,” he reports, “and it’s very comfortable here.” Two lux is 10 to 20 times the illuminance of a full moon. We can see each other clearly, and a nearby sign assures us that closed-circuit television (CCTV) is in operation for safety’s sake.  
我今晚的同伴是本地照明设计师西蒙·索普，他蹲在纳尔逊塔尖附近的阴影中，手持测光表。“两勒克斯，”他报告道，“这里非常舒适。”两勒克斯是满月亮度的10到20倍。我们能彼此清晰看见，附近一块告示牌保证闭路电视（CCTV）正在运行以保障安全。  

Light designer Simon Thorp uses a luxmeter to measure the intensity of light emitted by a street lamp in St. James’s Park, London.  
照明设计师西蒙·索普使用勒克斯表测量伦敦圣詹姆斯公园路灯的光强。  

Trafalgar Square captures the relationship between lighting and darkness that exists in almost every city, suburb, small town, and village around the world. The lighting here is a mishmash of old technologies and new, of shadow and glare, the ornamental gas lamps fronting the National Gallery all but washed out by the LEDs inside modern versions of traditional “brass and glass” fixtures a few meters away—21st-century technology housed in 19th-century designs.  
特拉法加广场捕捉了全球几乎每个城市、郊区、小镇和村庄中照明与黑暗的关系。此处的照明是旧技术与新、阴影与眩光的混杂：国家 Gallery 前的装饰性煤气灯几乎被几米外现代“黄铜玻璃”传统灯具内的LED灯淹没——21世纪的技术装在19世纪的设计中。  

The ugly truth of artificial lighting today is that in parts of London, as in many cities around the world, lighting levels are excessive, with unshielded illumination blasting in all directions. And the irony is that too much light invites danger: It creates shadows, impedes our vision, and gives the illusion, without the reality, of safety.  
当今人工照明的丑陋真相是，在伦敦部分地区乃至全球许多城市，照明水平过度，未屏蔽的光线向四面八方散射。讽刺的是，过多的光会招致危险：它制造阴影、阻碍视线，并制造安全的幻觉而非现实。  

Thorp notes that modern CCTV cameras are “pretty great” even at low-light levels, while harsh light makes it hard for both human eyes and digital sensors to see.  
索普指出，现代闭路电视摄像头在低光水平下“相当出色”，而刺眼的光线却让人眼和数字传感器都难以看清。  

“The more bad light we add, the more bad light we think we need,” says Thorp. “We can’t see because of the light we’ve added. And it makes areas that were perfectly okay seem darker.”  
“我们添加的劣质光越多，我们就越认为自己需要更多劣质光，”索普说，“我们因已添加的光而无法看清。它让原本完全正常的区域显得更暗。”  

Among the costs of this excess, the most alarming may be its toll on human health (and that of other animals) by disrupting circadian rhythms, impeding the production of melatonin, and contributing to sleep disorders that are tied to every major modern disease. New research shows that increased exposure to blue light from LEDs is having “substantial biological impacts” such as suppression of the sleep hormone melatonin and an increased risk for obesity, certain cancers, and type 2 diabetes.  
在这种过度照明带来的代价中，最令人震惊的可能是对人类（及其他动物）健康的损害：它扰乱昼夜节律、阻碍褪黑激素分泌，并导致与所有主要现代疾病相关的睡眠障碍。新研究表明，LED蓝光暴露增加正在产生“重大生物影响”，如抑制睡眠激素褪黑激素，并增加肥胖、某些癌症和2型糖尿病的风险。  

A panoramic view from the Eiffel Tower looks down on the illuminated Champ de Mars gardens [center] leading toward the École Militaire, with the tall silhouette of the Tour Montparnasse visible on the Paris skyline.  
从埃菲尔铁塔俯瞰的全景图中，灯光照亮的战神广场花园[中心]通向军事学院，巴黎天际线上可见蒙帕纳斯大楼的 Tall 轮廓。  

I have come to London and Paris—which led the way in the expansion of public street lighting in the 19th century—because they embody both the current enormity of the problem as well as a future certain to be lit by trillions of chips: controllable, tunable, and energy-efficient LEDs.  
我来到伦敦和巴黎——它们在19世纪引领了公共街道照明的扩张——因为它们既体现了当前问题的严重性，也预示着一个必将由数万亿芯片照亮的未来：可控、可调、节能的LED灯。  

Living with artificial light at night  
与夜间人工光共存  

The standard justification for nighttime illumination is public safety. Lighting experts’ term for the phenomenon is “artificial light at night.” While people won’t often admit it, the desire for light at night seems to stem from a primal fear of the dark. Darkness is where the bad guys hide. And if dark is bad and light is good, then more light can only be better.  
夜间照明的标准理由是公共安全。照明专家将这种现象称为“夜间人工光”。虽然人们往往不愿承认，但对夜间光的需求似乎源于对黑暗的原始恐惧。黑暗是坏人藏身之处。如果黑暗是坏的而光是好的，那么更多的光只会更好。  

This assumption has guided our use of nighttime light for hundreds of years. And yet, high-lumen output doesn’t necessarily correlate to a reduction in crime, research has found. In other words, if we relied on the data as much as we do our primal anxieties and paused those anxieties long enough to learn how light and darkness interact, our nights would almost certainly be lighted differently—especially now that we have the extraordinary technology that is the light-emitting diode.  
这一假设已指导我们使用夜间光照数百年。然而，研究发现高流明输出并不一定与犯罪减少相关。换言之，如果我们像依赖原始焦虑一样依赖数据，并足够长时间地暂停这些焦虑以了解光与黑暗如何互动，我们的夜晚几乎肯定会以不同方式被照亮——尤其是现在我们拥有发光二极管这项非凡技术。  

A 19th-century gas lamp [white square] manufactured by William Sugg & Co. next to a pedestrian path in Trafalgar Square, along with the architectural floodlighting on the neoclassical facade of the National Gallery, showcase the interplay between modern and historic lighting systems.  
特拉法加广场人行道旁由 William Sugg & Co. 制造的19世纪煤气灯[白框]，以及国家美术馆新古典主义立面上的建筑泛光灯，展示了现代与历史照明系统的相互作用。  

The first visible red light-emitting diode was invented by Nick Holonyak in 1962, but LED lighting technology took several decades to develop, before exploding in recent years. Just a decade ago, LED streetlamps were rare. By 2019, more than half of U.S. streetlights were LEDs, and that number is predicted to top 90 percent by 2030. Similar uptake has occurred around the world, even in developing countries, where inexpensive Chinese-made LED fixtures are increasingly common.  
第一个可见红光发光二极管由尼克·何伦亚克于1962年发明，但LED照明技术历经数十年发展，直到近年才爆发。仅十年前，LED路灯还很罕见。到2019年，美国超过一半的路灯已是LED，预计到2030年这一比例将超过90%。全球范围内也出现了类似普及，甚至在发展中国家，廉价的中国制造LED灯具正变得越来越普遍。  

This rapid global migration to LEDs represents a shift in the fundamental physics of how we illuminate our world. From oil lamps and candles to gas lamps, early examples of artificial light at night relied on a burning wick, an incredibly inefficient way to create light. An incandescent bulb is effectively a heater that happens to produce light as a by-product, so it squanders nearly all of its energy as heat.  
全球迅速转向LED代表了我们照亮世界的基本物理学的转变。从油灯、蜡烛到煤气灯，早期夜间人工光依赖燃烧灯芯，这是一种效率极低的光创造方式。白炽灯泡实际上是一个产生光作为副产品的加热器，因此它几乎将所有能量浪费为热量。
